abstract: Emergent trees play a disproportionate role in tropical forest function,
  contributing substantially to aboveground biomass and biodiversity. However, the
  low density, long lifespans, and uneven distributions of emergent tree species across
  landscapes have limited our understanding of their sensitivity to anthropogenic
  disturbances. Here, we developed and tested a framework using multiple remote sensing
  sources to monitor target crowns of the emergent Dipteryx panamensis across northern
  Costa Rica and southern Nicaragua. In this analysis, we evaluated four sensor configurations
  combining open-access optical and radar datasets. In contrast to earlier limited-area
  studies, spectral-only models performed poorly. Models combining spectral and SAR
  datasets achieved higher accuracies, demonstrating the complementarity of datasets
  for consistent emergent crown detection at landscape scales. Our all-data model
  generated a highly accurate crown-level distribution map for D. panamensis (F1 score = 0.94).
  We found that emergent crowns persisted in both intact forests and pastures, raising
  questions about the long-term survival of these populations under continuing disturbances.
  Across Costa Rica and Nicaragua, we observed higher densities within forest environments,
  with sharp declines toward the edges and non-forest areas, indicating that persistence
  is spatially structured by forest integrity and land-use context. Higher overall
  densities in Nicaragua indicate that land-use history (e.g., frontier age) or other
  anthropogenic factors may shape the persistence of emergent species at a landscape
  scale. Beyond D. panamensis, our findings demonstrate that integrating free moderate-resolution
  optical and SAR datasets enables target crown species mapping at landscape scales
  and provides a scalable framework for monitoring other ecologically significant
  or threatened tropical trees.
